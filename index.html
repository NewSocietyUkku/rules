Explanation of sounds in the primary alphabet



puente sounds:

These are vowels.



oculto sounds.:

These are consonants that can replace the vowels in a syllable in any language. For example, ‘pet’ can become ‘prt’



estancado sounds:

These are consonants that can be placed between a grupo sound and a oculto sound. For example, the ‘k’ in ‘skate.’



grupo sounds:

These are consonants that can be placed before any other sound mentioned above, at the start of a syllable. For example, the ‘s’ in ‘snake.’



pronouncing words



All words are pronouncing the same way in which they are written. The direction that a letter is in, tells you when a new syllable begins. Each letter in the alphabet attached, is written in the standard direction. Letters are normally in this direction. However, at the start of every syllable, they are flipped horizontally.

The above is true only for letters in the primary alphabet.



The direction the language is written in



The script first start being writing from left to right. However, when one reaches the end of the page, they simply change the standard direction of writing from left to right, to, right to left. Therefore they will start from the right of the page and move left, flipping all the letters horizontally.

When one has reached the left of the page, then they start writing from left to right, and so one.

As with before, each letter of the primary alphabet that is to start a syllable, goes in the opposite direction to the standard direction. So if the standard direction currently is from right to left, then they letter will be flipped to left to right, at the start of a syllable.

There exists another way of written which consists of writting first from top to bottom and then to bottom to top and then bottom to top. The letters are still written from left to right or right to left, despute being written in a vertical manner. When one writes frm left to right then they assume the main direction of main direction of the characters from left to right and when written from bottom to top, then the main direction is assumed to be from right to left.

This allows for more flexibility, as one can choose whether to write vertically or horizontally.



definición sounds

In the new language each word needs to start with a definición sound. These sounds only exist at the start of words. Therefore you know when a new word begins in a sentence, by hearing the definición sound.

The definición sounds tell you more about the word (what type of word is it.) The details of what each definición sound tells you, can be seen in the alphabet for definición sounds.

Sound changes that arise due to nasal and tonal sounds



The new language is tonal as it has one tone, a rising tone. This tone is shown by making the puente sound increase in pitch as you are saying it. Some puente sounds are said normally and some are said using a rising tone. You can say this tone the same way you would as in Mandarin (Chinese), or you could do it the same way as in English, and the Romance languages (e.g. Italian, Spanish, etc), when you raise the tone to ask a question. For example, ‘now?’. It doesn’t matter which one of these two raising tones you use. The language is flexible. The two are treated as the same sound.

However, there exist one exception to this rule where rising tones change. The two R@ and L@ sounds, are said at the bottom of the throat. It is difficult to pronounce R@ or L@, after a rising tone. For that reason, as a rule, if a tonal puente sound appears before a R@ or L@, in the same syllable; then it changes from a raising tone to a tone that rises then descends. In other words, you simply lower the tone at the end as to make the R@ and L@ sounds easier to pronounce.

Along with R@ and L@ sounds, the new language also has a nasal R sound and a nasal L sound. In order to make it easier to say these nasal oculto sounds, if they appear after a puente sound, then the puente sound before it needs to change. The puente sound can either start normal and become nasal OR start nasal and end nasal. Which one the speaker chooses depends on whatever they feel most comfortable with. If the puente sound is tonal, then the rising tone only starts rising after the puente sound becomes tonal.

As with the two rules above, they don’t need to be practiced constantly, as one will find their mouth will naturally speak that way when saying such words.

Below are two further exceptions.

1. If you have a nasal oculto sound, followed by a puente sound, then followed by a nasal puente sound (e.g. R̃ER̃), then the puente sound becomes nasal (i.e. R̃ẼR̃). The puente sound starts and ends as a nasal sound. The new grupo sound is said at the top of the mouth. However, if you have only a nasal oculto sound, followed by a puente sound, then the nasal oculto sound has no effect on the puente sound. You need both nasal oculto sounds, or only one after the puente sound, for it to have an effect on the puente sound.

2. This also applies for R@/L@ but instead the puente sound is said as a low pitched tonal sound. e.g. if you have ‘R@aL@’ then you would pronounce the ‘a’ with a lower tone (You will find that you naturally do this while speaking). As with the rule with sounds ending on a R@/L@, if the puente sound is tonal (e.g. R@a?L@), then it changes to a raising and lowering tone. The only difference is that it starts at a much lower tone to begin with to match the R@/L@ sound coming before it.

Basics of the new language



Every word is a description, meaning that it describes something. A word can either be describing another word in the sentence or be describing something outside of the sentence.

When words describe something outside of the sentence, the order of those words do not matter. For example, the sentence ‘[cute][smelly][happy]’ is equivalent to ‘[happy][cute][smelly]’, or whatever order you wish to say the words in.

However, when a word describes another word, then the order does matter. It needs to be either placed in front of a word it is describing or in front of word/list of words, that is describing the same thing, which it is describing.

For example,
the sentence ‘[happy]{greatly}[cute][smelly]’ is different from the sentence ‘[happy][cute]{greatly}[smelly].

The first sentence describes something as being very happy, while being cute and smelly, at the same time. The second sentence on the other hand, describes something as being very cute, while describing it as happy and smelly, at the same time.

The sentence‘[smelly][smelly]’ is simply a repetition of ‘smelly.’ It has no further meaning. However, for grammatical purposes explained later in the document, you may be required to do such repetition.

The sentence ‘[smelly]{smelly}’ on the other hand, describes something as having a specific kind of smelliness. A smelly kind of smelliness.

If you have more than one word, describing the same word, which of the words appears first is not of importance.

For example,
the sentence ‘[anger]{great}{revengeful}{evil}’ is the same as ‘[anger]{revengeful}{great}{evil}.



Mixing word



If you want to describe something as being a mix between two qualities, you would use the 'gRan’ word.

For example,
in the sentence ‘[bat][human]’ something is being described as being both a human and a bat. Therefore that sentence would imply that being a bat does not contradict being a human. However, the sentence ‘[gRan]{bat}{human}’ describes something as being half human and half bat. Maybe a human with bat like wings or something like the film ‘Batman’. The thing it is describing could be neither human nor bat, but shares qualities of both.



The meaningless word



The word ‘glar’ has no meaning on it’s own. For example, [animal]{glar} is the same as saying [animal].

This sound picks up the meaning of the sounds added to it. e.g. if a sufijo sound is added to it then it picks up the meaning of that sufijo sound.

This word may appear useless, but it becomes important for grammatical purposes.

What a sufijo sound is, will be explained later in this document.

The clause



In the new language, a clause is all the words that describe the word that describes something out side of the sentence, or describes a word that leads to describing that. For example, the sentence below consists of two clauses.

[gRan]{human} ^male^ {bat} [black] {evil} {scary} {strange} {wonderful} {pretty}

clause 1= [gRan]{human} ^male^ {bat}

(gRan is the mixing word explained earlier in the document. So therefore this clause describes something as being half human and half bat, and specifically describes the type of human as being a male human ‘a man’)̧

clause 2=[black] {evil} {scary} {strange} {wonderful} {pretty}

this clause describes the thing as being black. Specifically a evil type of black, a scary type of black, a strange type of black, a wonderful type of black, and ultimately a pretty type of black.

It is OK for a sentence to only contain one clause. It is also OK for the for a clause to contain only one word. e.g. [cat]



Adding meaning to entire clause or sentence

‘[gL@a?Lnsh’] {only partly} [black] [green] [orange] [transparent] [purple] [yellow] [red] {strange}’

‘{gL@a?Lnsh} ^wonderfully^ { [black] {partly} [cat] {happpy} [spy] {secretly}’ = ‘[black] {partly} ^wonderfully^ [cat] {happy} ^wonderfully^ [spy] {secretly} {wonderfully>’

‘gLe?Lns’ is the same as above but only works within the clause and works cross all the words in the clause regardless of that level. e.g. ‘[black] {intensely} {strange} ^intensely^ ^wonderfully^ {(^intensely^)} [cat] {happy}’= ‘[gLe?Lns’] {intensely} [black] {strange} ^wonderfully^ [cat] {happy}’

Notice that the ‘[black]’ this time is not described as an intense kind of black. Only a black that is intensely strange but at the same time wonderfully (intensely wonderfully) strange.



Punctuation



There exist only three punctuation marks that can be placed at the end of a sentence. This is the full stop ‘.’ (used for statements) [they look exactly like normal full stops but instead are put in the middle of the line, as opposed to the bottom], the question mark ‘i’ [the question mark is drawn like a lower case i / an upside down exclamation mark] (used for questions), and the final is an exclamation mark ‘!’ (used for commands) [it should be noted that unlike English, these do not change the pronunciation as used with any command or questions.

For example, ‘please do come eat whatever you like!’ does not mean that the person is shouting/showing emotion. It simply means that a command was giving.

The above punctuation is optional. They may be included or left out based upon the writers preferences. Although not necessary, the intended purpose of punctuation is to make reading easier and quicker.



Questions and commands



In order to use the punctuation above you need to know if something is a question, exclamation or statement. The new language has different rules for what is considered a question, exclamation or statement, than other languages. Luckily the rules are fairly straight foreword. Everything is a statement if it does not contain one of the following 6 command/question words (listed below), then the sentence is a statement.

If you ask someone to ‘go fetch me a tool’ or ‘drink water’ this is treated as a statement. You are simply explaining more about yourself as a beggar, and the type of begging you are doing is a ‘fetch me a tool’ type of begging

e.g. [me] [strongly begging/insisting] {you} {bring} ^to me^ The above would be only a description of yourself and therefore is not a command and therefore would end in a full stop as opposed to an exclamation mark.

There exist three of these ‘please’ words (to show what other languages may refer to as a command) gR*uns=please/asking to (but suggest if you say ‘no’ it is not a problem. Not insisting.

gR@antsh=begging for something. Being insistent.
gLa?s=demanding that someone do something. This word is not intended to be rude.

All three words only describe the type of ‘beggar’ and what they are begging for. They do not turn a sentence into a command.

There exist 6 command/question words. There are Three categories. Each category has a word for yes/no questions and one word for general questions. All commands in the new language are in reality questions. Kind of like how you would say ‘tell me what time it is’ in English.

The first category is for rhetorical question. Sentences containing these are statements and therefore end with a full stop. These questions are used when no answer is needed. e.g. when a teacher explains to a class and asks “Therefore, what is 5x”. They do not want the students to give an answer. Or if your boss asks you “Is this all you done for five weeks”. A response is not needed.

1.
For questions with a yes/no answers gLe?tsh
2.
For general questions gRas

The second category is used for questions out of curiosity. Like the above, the speaker is asking but they not expected an answer from someone. However, the speaker does indeed want to know the answer out of curiosity. When a boss asks “Is this all you done for five weeks” he knows the answer and isn’t curios about what it might be.

If someone thinks to themselves “what is baby powder actually made of?”, they aren’t asking anyone for an answer but at the same time, they do want to know the answer. Most of the time, this is done when the speaker thinks to himself/speaks to himself but there are other situations where it may occur.

If a sentence uses one of the two words found in the second category, then it ends in a question mark.

For questions with a yes/no answers gROARz

For general questions gLOA?Rz

[yesterday] [cat] [black] to [gLOA?Rz] {time} [cat] [black] what time was the cat black

Adding focus to a sentence In the following sentence ‘[cat] [black]’, you describe something as being a cat and black. However, there may be times when you want to put focus/add new information, on a specific word. For example, if someone says the cat is white, you do not want to just say ‘[cat] [black]’. You want to say that it is specifically the blackness that is new information. The way you would do this is with the word ‘gLOAL*’

[cat][black] {gLOAL*}=The cat is black

[black] {gLOAL*} [cat] = The black thing is a cat

This is also important for the 6 question/command words used above.

For example, if a teacher were to ask a rhetorical question a in the example below

e.g.
Student “The bird is inside.”

Teacher asking rhetorically “where is the bird?”

student “[bird] [inside]”

Teacher “[bird] [gRas] {place/location} {gLOAL*}”

Clearly the teacher could have simply asked “[bird] [gRas] {place/location}”, but by adding “{gLOAL*}”, he is making it clear that where the bird is the focus of the sentence.

There is also a no/non sufijo sound (sufijo sounds are explained later in the document) that confirms that a word is not a ‘gLOAL*’ but the vast majority of the time this will not be needed as you should be able to understand this from both the context and where the ‘gLOAL*’ word is placed. Despite this, below exist an example using this no/non sufijo.

[cat][black] {gLOAL*}=The cat is black

[cat]{gLOAL*+no/no sufijo}[black] {gLOAL*}=The cat is black

[cat]{gLOAL*+no/no sufijo}[black] =The cat is black

In the third example above, it is clear that the ‘[black]’ is the gLOAL*, even without the word stating it, because the sentence is simple. In more complex sentences it isn’t so clear. For that reason it is recommended that you use ‘gLOAL*’ instead of ‘gLOAL*+no/no sufijo’

e.g. [black]{gLOAL*+no/no sufijo} [sick] [skinny] [meoing] {annoyingly}

It only makes sense to use the above if all the the other qualities would need a ‘gLOAL’ word and you do not wish to write it all.

Hi and How are you



In the new language there are different words for hello and goodbye based upon whether you know the person. There is only one word for ‘how are you?’ Therefore, regardless of which ‘hello’ you use, you would still use the same ‘how are you’ afterwards. These two words go together in the same sentence, they are not split between two different sentences. The reason for this is when you say ‘hello’ and ‘how are you’ in one sentence, what you are saying directly translated is, you are described as a receiver of ‘hello-ness’ and a receiver of ‘how are you-ness’

For example, ‘GRuRn’ is used to say hello to someone you met before and ‘gLu?Rn’ is used to say ‘how are you’.

[gRuRn] [gLu?Rn]

It is also worth noting that the ‘how are you’ word does not only ask how you are feeling. It asks how are you feeling, what is around you/happening in the world/what are you currently experiencing.

You could respond with something like “I’m feeling ill today” OR “The clouds look a little strange today” OR “The president just dead. I saw it on my phone”

You could respond simply with ‘gROARnss’. This is a word for any command/question that you wish to pass away without actually answering and isn’t seen as rude to use. You can use it here, despite the fact that ‘gLu?Rn’ isn’t a question, but rather a description. It is describing yourself as receiving ‘gLu?Rn’.



How to describe things



As mentioned before, all words in the new language are either describing another word or describing something outside of the sentence. The way you would know what the word describes is by the word form.



There are 3 different forms a word can be in.

The first form describes something outside of the sentence For example, in the sentence ‘[anger]{revengeful}^greatly^’ the ‘[anger]’ is a word in the first from, as it describes something as being angry.

The second form describes a word that is in the first form.

For example, in the sentence

‘[anger]{revengeful} ^greatly^ the ‘{revengeful}’

is a word in the second form, as it describes the ‘[anger]’ (which is a word in the first form) as being a specific kind of anger. It describes it as being a revengeful type of anger.

The third form describes a word that is in the second form.

For example, in the sentence

‘[anger]{revengeful}^greatly^’ the ‘^greatly^’ is a word in the third form, as it describes the ‘{resentful-ness}’

(which is a word in the second form) as being a specific kind of revenge. It describes it as being a great/extreme type of resentfulness.



How the three word types are spoken and written



First word form

You know what word form it is by where the syllable is. If the first syllable remains unbroken, then that means that the word is in the first form. e.g. ‘grut’ OR ‘PRE*Rnss - sNR*OAnss’ (this word is 2 syllables long because the first syllable is unbroken’)



second word form

If a word is in the second form, then the first syllable is broken into two syllables. The second syllable, now starts with a puente sound

e.g. ‘gr - ut’ OR ‘PR - -ER*nss - sNR*OAnss’ (this word is 3 syllables long because the first syllable was broken down into two separate syllables’)



Third word form

For the third word form, you keep it the same as the second word form but move the puente sound to fit between the grupo sound and the first oculto sound.

e.g. ‘gur - t’ OR ‘PER - R*nss – sNR*OAnss’’

The above examples wouldn’t actually be correct as we need to now join the broken syllable to make one syllable (If this is not possible due to it having the two oculto sounds, then the extra oculto sound needs to form another syllable.

eg. ‘gurt’ (is the correct version) OR ‘PERnss – R* – sNR*OAnss’’

(Being able to recognize third form words with double oculto sounds, is probably one of the hardest parts of the language. However, if you learn a few examples off by heart, you brain will be able to follow the pattern. Due to the way the new language is structured, learning words is easy as you can hear how many words are in a sentence. This should be more than sufficient to make up for the time a student has spent to study both, the double oculto sound word, and it’s third word form.)

In writing, letters in the primary alphabet have direction, showing which way they are facing. As a rule, any sound in the alphabet that is placed at the start of a syllable, is written with it’s letter facing in the opposite direction. Therefore you can read where the syllables are and so you know the word form while reading text in the new language.

Due to the fact that the writing system has no assigned letter for the ‘R’ sound, as it needs to attach to another letter: the ‘R’ sound attaches to the last sound of the previous syllable in writing. Due to the fact that it is not possible to pronounce a oculto sound at the end of a syllable, after the final stagement sound/grupo, it is clear that the oculto sound forms a new syllable.

e.g. PERnss – R* – sNR*OAnss = PERnssR* – sNR*OAnss (when written).

Words that describe words in the third word form As mentioned before, there are only three word forms that exist in the new language. If you wish to describe a word in the third type, there exists no forth word form, to allow you to do this.

For example, in the sentence ‘[anger]{revengeful}^greatly^’ if you want to describe the greatness/extremeness of the revengefulness, as being only slightly great, there exists no other word to do this.

The way we get around the problem mentioned above, is by use of another word ‘gLa?n’, to assist us with this. ‘gLa?n’ is always said in the first word form, in this circumstance. The words after this will begin in the first word form again

For example, in the sentence

‘[anger]{revengeful}^greatly^ [gLa?n],[slightly]{probably}’

, the word ‘gLa?n’ allows you to describe words of the third form. The slightly-ness is being described as something that is believed to be true, but could be wrong, and the greatly-ness is being described as only being slightly great. The [slightly] word be said like any other word in the first word form and the {probably} word like any other word in the second word form.

The way you would show that the ‘gLa?n’ no longer applies, is by putting it in the second form. This is to indicate that all words in the first word form going forward, are describing something outside of the sentence.

For example, in the sentence [anger]{revengeful}^greatly^[gLa?n][slightly]{probably}{gL - a?n}[animal]’ the animal (not the ‘greatness’/‘extremeness’) is being described as both being angry and being animal like. The rest of the words in the sentence simply further explain what type of anger the animal has.

For sentences with many different ‘gLa?n ‘words, it can be difficult to keep track of what the speaker is saying. Therefore, when you say ‘gLa?n’ in the third form, then it resets the rest of the sentence to how it would be, if no ‘ gLa?n were said at all. This is different to ‘gLa?n’ in the second word form, as ‘ gLa?n’ in the second word form only brings it down three levels.

In other words, all words in the first form, said after the ‘ga?Ln’ (third form), are describing something external to the sentence.

However, in reality, such deep description upon descriptions, isn’t likely to exist. In most cases a single ‘gLa?n’ would be sufficient.



Syllable stress (names are an exception to this rule)



In English, certain syllables are stressed and unstressed. In the new language, stress is not used at all. What the new language does have however, is have syllable pairs. syllables inside a word appear in pairs of two for each word.

e.g.
*gr-ut* *o-mut* - *ta* - *plOAlf*

Notice that in the above example, ‘*ta*’ and *plOAlf* are said apart, as opposed to together as ‘*ta-plOAlf*’. This is because ‘ploaf’ is a new word and the first syllable of a new word always attaches to the next syllable of the same word, and never to the previous syllable of the word before it. Syllable pairs never cross between words.

Sometimes (but not often) it may be difficult to say two syllables together, then it is OK to split a pair into two. However, the other pairs must stay the same.

e.g.

‘*sPRE*Rnss* - *sNR*OAnss*’ instead of ‘*svPRE*Rnss - sNR*OAns*’

‘*sPRE*Rnss* - *sNR*OAnss* - *o-mut* - *ta* - *plOAlf*

another example would be

‘*sprelks-met* - *ta*’ = ‘*sprelks* - *met* - *ta*’

Please note that it would be incorrect to say

*sprelks* - *met - ta*

This is wrong because ‘ta’ is the third syllable of the word and therefore needs to form a pair with the fourth term, not the second term (‘met’).



Word extensions



No word in the new language (even non official words) may end in a puente sound.

For any word, you can add a word extensions to the end of a word, and these word extensions end with a puente sound. These word extensions can consist only of a puente sound.

e.g. ‘pLuL*T-u’ is not a word extension OR ‘grut-u’ s not a word extension

Any other syllable consisting of letters from the primary alphabet and ending in a ‘puente sound’, is a word extensions, with the exception of diphthongs.

For example, ‘sgRaz’ can become ‘sgRaz-sa’, ‘sgRaz-stre’, ‘sgRaz-ru’, or any other combination. How ‘sa/su/sh^u/ect’ and ‘ra/lu/l@e/ect’ are drawn can be seen where the other examples of how the other sounds in the alphabet are drawn. You will find these images attached in the same folder that this document is in.



Diphthongs are not allowed



e.g. you cannot say ‘gruz-tai’, since diphthongs cannot be said.

Only one of these extensions are allowed to be added. So you cannot say ‘gruz-ta-ne’ for example.

These extensions have no meaning of their own and may be added whenever the speaker wishes. However, they are needed in order to add certain grammatical features to words.



Word tenses



By default, all words in the [Ukku Language] contain no tenses. However, tenses can be added onto a word. The way you would give tense to a word is by lengthening the sound in a word. You simply say that sound for a slightly longer period of time, than the other sounds.

In writing, you would show a lengthened sound by repeating a letter (writing it twice). The letter being repeated, faces the standard direction. Otherwise it would be read as two different syllables if facing the opposite direction to the standard direction. For example, ‘grenn’ is a tense, while ‘gren-n’ is a word with the ‘n’ sound said twice at the end.

Tenses are determined in terms of the speaker or event. For example, if talking about yesterday, it is normal to switch back to present tense to describe things that happened yesterday and what happens today, would be in the future tense.

In other words, the tense is determined not by the current time line, but the time at which the events talked about, are taking place. If no other time line has been mentioned, it is assumed that the speaker is talking about the present moment he/she is talking in.

The future tense is said by lengthening the first oculto sound. The third form is said differently and an example is shown below.

e.g. ‘gRRoats-stra'

OR (if the word is in the third word form)

‘gRRut-stra’

The present tense is said by lengthening the puente sound.

e.g. ‘grOAOAts-stra’

OR (if the word is in the third word form)

‘grUUt-stra’

The past tense is said by lengthening the first stagement sound/last sound of the syllable.

e.g ‘groatss-stra’

OR (if the word is in the third word form)

‘gruTT-stra’

For the rest of the tenses you need to add a word extension, to the end of the word.

A lengthened estancado sound in the word extension, shows that an action just happened (e.g. I just ate.) For example, ‘groats-sttra’ or ‘groats-nnu’’

A lengthened oculto sound in the word extension, shows that an action is still in the process of being done (e.g. I am eating) For example, ‘groats-strra’ or ‘groats-rro’

A lengthened puente sound in the word extension, shows that an action was just about to be completed (e.g. I was about to start eating.) For example, ‘groats-straa’ or ‘groats-roo’

A lengthened groupererble sound in the word extension, show an hypercritical tense For example, ‘gref-sstra’ or ‘gref-sse’

This hypercritical tense is used for something that has not happened yet but could happen or could have happened. For example, ‘I would have liked that.’ vs ‘I like that.’ OR ‘If I would have been there’ vs ‘I was there’



sufijo sounds



All sufijo sounds, and their meanings, are listed in the sufijo sounds alphabet. sufijo sounds are added to words to give further meaning to the word. For example, the sentence ‘[dead]-brought about/because of, sufijo sound-me-[him]’ means that I killed him. Literally it means that the man is dead because of me.

sufijo sounds can either be added inside the word (between syllables) or at the end. The meaning of the sufijo sound changes based upon if the sound is added inside the word or at the end of it. The meaning for each sufijo sound for inside a word vs for at the end, can be seen by the spreadsheet attached in the sufijo sound alphabet.

sufijo sounds act like their own syllable and therefore are written in a manner that does not show direction (they are identical when flipped horizontally).

If a word only consists of one syllable, then you will need to add a word extension at the end, to stop the sufijo sound from being at the end of the word.

For example, ‘gruz’ will need to become ‘gruz-na’ as to allow a sufijo sound inside of the word ‘gruz-sufijo sound-na’

The nature of the language can create many syllables to say things in English that would require less syllables; part of the reason for this is the way the language breaks a syllable into two words for the second form.

To say 'becaus' ypu need 4 syllables, and to ask why sdomeone has done something you would say 'you did it because what reason'. The 'what reason' is another 4 syllables'. That means that you would need 8 syllables to say why. Clearly this is not practical.

To get round such probems we use the 'e' suffix sound, whcih we add to the end of the word. This joins a few words as a new two syllable word, containting the first syllable of the first word of the phrase and the last syyllable of the phrase as a the final syllable

e.g.

The above phrase ends in a 2 syablle word. If the word phrase however, ends with a 1 word then you do the same but just make it two words seperately

e.g.

Because the above is a single syable word you automatically know that the next word is not a seperate word and form part fo the shortning



pronombre sounds



Attached you will find the 8 pronombre sounds (e.g. I/myself).

For example, Boy + ‘me pronombre sound’ describes something as being both me and a boy. In other words it says “I am a boy”

If a pronombre sound appears directly after a sufijo sound, then it describes the sufijo sound instead.

For example
‘dead’ + ‘because of sufijo sound’ + ‘me pronombre sound’ = dead because of me (it does not mean I am dead because of me)

OR

‘dead’ + ‘us including you pronombre sound’ + ‘because of sufijo sound’ + ‘me pronombre sound’ = we are dead because of me (i.e. I killed us)

It is possible for more than one pronombre sound to appear after a sufijo sound. As long as they appear directly after the sufijo sound they are applicable to that sufijo sound

For examples
‘dead’ + ‘it pronombre sound’ ‘because of sufijo sound’ + ‘me pronombre sound’ ‘you pronombre sound’

It is dead because of you and me.

As with sufijo sounds, the meaning of the pronombre sound changes based upon it’s position in the word. If it appears at the end of a word, then it means that it is describing something outside of the sentence. If it however, appears in between a word, then it describes that was a being a specific type of word with those pronombre qualities.

For example,
[happy-me]=I am happy-me [happy-me-ta]=a specific type of happiness that is a ‘me kind of’ happiness.

Another example [dog] {happy-me}=a dog that is described as being both happy and ‘me’ like.

Adding numbers to words Like with pronombre sounds, numbers also have different meanings based upon whether they are at the end of, or inside a word.

If a number appears at the end of a word, then it means that the number is applied to quantity. For example, 5 sheep, 2 cows, etc.

Inside the word then it describes the object as being a type of object like that number.

e.g.
plOAfl5=5 chairs plOAfl5ta=a chair that is ‘5 like’

Each sufijo sound in the alphabet is divided into ‘not applicable’, ‘quantitative’, ‘specific’ and ‘exceptions’ categories.

For all sufijo sounds in the ‘exceptions’‘specific’, and ‘not applicable’ categories, the numbering has no effect. However, for numbering in the ‘quantitative’ category, zero and all positive numbers show the extent.

It shows the degree/extent/how strong a word is.

e.g.
very strong/only partly strong. (The way you can think of this is 900 being very high and 0 being nothing)

If a number appears directly after a sufijo sound/after a pronombre appearing after a sufijo, then it means that it applies to that sufijo. As a rule it can only apply to the ‘quantitative’ (because that is the only one that makes logical sense).

e.g.
plOAlf+insult sufijo+900=a very strong insult to say that something is a chair. It doesn’t mean that you have 900 insulting chairs.

Repeating words in a sentence At times it may be useful to repeat a word twice, as to prevent confusion and ambiguity.

E.g. while using many sufijo sounds in the same word

[grut]+me+cause sufijo sound+you + compliment + 900 = [grut]+me+cause sufijo sound+you[grut] compliment + 900

Repeating a word has no effect on the meaning of that word.

e.g.
[grut][grut][grut]=[grut]

The two sentences above have exactly the same meaning



Conjunction between sentences



‘ggLa?nts-ta?t’ and ‘gLOA?T-ta?t’, as can be seen in the dictionary for the language, both have the same meaning of ‘and’. Unlike with conjunctions in English, these conjunctions are used to show the end of a sentence while showing relation of the new sentence to the old one.

e.g.
[cat][dead][gLOA?T-ta?t]!?. [mother][crying] = the cat is dead and the mother is crying.

‘ggLa?nts-ta?t’ means exactly the same thing.

e.g.
[cat][dead][ggLa?nts-ta?t]!?. [mother][crying] = the cat is dead and the mother is crying.

However, when another word is described with them, they don’t mean the same thing as each other. The extra word explains/justifies why the conjunction is used.

e.g.
[victory] [ggLa?nts-ta?t]!?. [I short] = I won and I was short

[victory] [ggLa?nts-ta?t] {talent}!?. [I short] = I won despite the fact that I was short

The explanation/justification is ‘talent’. In other words the purpose of the conjunction is to further describe the talent.

As can be seen from the above, when a word further describes the [ggLa?nts-ta?t], it changes it’s meaning from ‘and’ to ‘despite’. When a word describes ‘gLOA?T-ta?t’ however, it changes it’s meaning to ‘but’.

e.g.
[person] [short] [gLOA?T-ta?t]!?. [not that short] = he is short and not that shortening

[person] [short] [gLOA?T-ta?t] {height}!?. [not that short] = he is short but not that shortening

The explanation/justification is ‘height’. In other words the purpose of the conjunction is to further describe the height.

If you describe the word using ‘gLaR’ (the meaningless word in the dictionary, then the meaning is changed again.

e.g. [cat][dead] [ggLa?nts-ta?t]!?. [hot][weather] = The cat is dead and the weather is hot

[cat][dead][ggLa?nts-ta?t] {gl-ar}!?. [hot][weather]= The cat is dead because the weather is hot.

There is no explanation/justification. In other words there is no purpose giving to justify the conjunction.

[person] [bad] [gLOA?T-ta?t]!?. [killer] = he is a bad person and he is a killer

[person] [bad] [gLOA?T-ta?t] {gl-ar}!?. [not a killer] = he is a bad person therefore he is a killer

There is no explanation/justification. In other words there is no purpose giving to justify the conjunction.

Each conjunction in the new language has an equivalent that isn’t used to join sentences but rather used to join words inside of that sentence. For example ‘gR*uRntsh’ is used as ‘and’ for words inside the sentence

e.g.[cat] [dog] [fighting] = something is both a cat and a dog and is fighting.

[gR*uRntsh] {cat} {dog} [fighting] = the cats and dogs were fighting.

Notice that like the mixing word, ‘{cat} {dog}’ appear in the second word form and describe the ‘and’ as a ‘cat and dog’ type of ‘and’.

Punctuation and spacing in the language



There are no spaces between words. Instead words are separated with a ‘/’ symbol (a straight line starting at the bottom left and moving to the top right, if the standard direction is from left to right and vice verse if from right to left).

(This ‘/’ sign is optional. One does not need to put it in as the definición sounds tell you when a new word begins. You can simply put the two words next to each other without a space, or ‘/’ [e.g. GlelzGrut]

However, a ‘/’ is recommended because it makes texts easier to read. With computers, you won’t need to put the ‘/’ as it will automatically generate it. Therefore giving the reader the option to read the text with ‘/’ or without it.)

Commas do not exist in the language. There only exists the three punctuation marks, used to indicate if it is a command, question or statement.

If a word doesn’t fit at the end of he page, there is no need to add any other marker. You would simply just write the rest of the letters of the word, on the line underneath it, in the opposite standard direction. It is clear from the fact that the letters do not start in a definición sound, that they are not forming a new word.



Showing medium



To show that something is a medium (e.g. medium height, not tall or short), one would use a combination of the ‘low quantity’ sufijoes and the ‘high quantity’ sufijoes.



Working with many

If you say Jack and Jill enjoy sleeping and eating, it is assumed that both of them enjoy both activities. The default assumption is always like that. You would need to add other words in to specify if you meant otherwise.

Another example would be the sentence ‘James and Marry observed the audience with their 4 eyes’ which would mean that they are some 4 eyed aliens, or something. If you say I have 23 homes with 13 swimming pools, it is assumed that each house has 13 pools in it. You will need to make use of other words, to clarify otherwise.

The word you would use for this is ‘sgR@a?Lns’

e.g.
[houses]23[ sgR@a?Lns] {pools}23=I have 23 houses and altogether they have 13 pools between them.



Subjective words



In the new language, all subjective words are represented by emotions. For example, there would be no word for beautiful. Instead there is a word for the feeling you get. You describe that thing as giving you that feeling.

e.g.
‘a giver/creator of beauty’

To say the above example you would use the first sufijo sound meaning ‘brought about by’.

To describe something a beautiful, you would use the ‘beauty feeling’ + the ‘creation/bring about’ sufijo. That means that it creates/brings about that feeling.

e.g.
[beauty feeling] + sufijo sound glottal k = it creates beauty = it is beautiful.

You may even add the habitual tense to this, if you want to specify that it’s not only a once off occurrence.
